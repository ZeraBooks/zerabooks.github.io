What a Properly Structured Bank Statement CSV Contains

The difference between a useful bank statement CSV and a raw export is structure. A raw PDF-to-text tool gives you all the characters on the page in reading order — which includes page headers, account numbers, branch addresses, and other non-transaction content mixed with transaction rows.

A proper bank statement CSV extraction identifies transaction rows specifically and outputs them in a standardized column format that any accounting software, spreadsheet, or reconciliation tool can consume directly.

Frequently Asked Questions

What columns does a bank statement CSV typically include?

A properly structured bank statement CSV includes: Date, Description, Debit, Credit, Balance, and optionally Reference/Check number and Category. Zera Books outputs all six in a standardized format compatible with Excel, accounting software CSV imports, and other tools.

Can Zera Books convert scanned bank statements to CSV?

Yes. Zera OCR processes scanned and image-based PDFs at 95%+ accuracy on financial documents before extraction. The resulting CSV is identical in structure to output from a digital PDF.

How is bank statement CSV different from a raw PDF text export?

A raw PDF text export gives you unstructured text — transaction data mixed with headers, footers, and formatting artifacts. Bank statement CSV extraction identifies transaction rows specifically and outputs them in clean columns: one row per transaction, with correctly identified dates, amounts, and descriptions.

What encoding does the CSV output use?

Zera Books exports UTF-8 encoded CSV by default, compatible with Excel, Google Sheets, and all major accounting platforms. Windows-1252 encoding is available for legacy systems that require it.
